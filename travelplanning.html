Travel Planning

Overview

As a part of COGS 102C, we focused on a problem surrounding a certain topic, which in this case was travel planning. We focused our problem on budgeting with college students. Through extensive research, we found that budgeting was a core problem in travel planning to college students. We developed various prototypes as solutions to help with budgeting, such as a visual tool for users to really see how much of their budget is being allocated to various aspects of their trip.

My Role

This project was done with 3 other classmates as a part of COGS 102C: Cognitive Design Studio. We each completed equal amounts of interviewing, research, and competitive analysis.

The Problem

We started out looking at travel planning for college students as our design opportunity. With this in mind, we went through a series of need-finding interviews, where we hoped to learn more about travel planning as an overall process and find out where people encountered problems. After these interviews, we aggregated our data and created an affinity diagram. Our affinity diagram led us to realize that there were a lot of problems we could tackle within travel planning and that we should narrow down our problem space.

We went through a second round of interviews to look at specifically how people planned. We had observed that travel planning was a very complicated process and we wanted to know what made import so complicated. Why did travel planning necessitate the usage of an abundance of different tools and was there any way we could improve how people planned their trips. We wanted to see what items they prioritized and why.

When we looked at our research again, we realized that we had misinterpreted our data. The complicated nature of travel planning was not the problem we were looking for. Rather, it was a symptom that resulted from trying to travel plan with a budget. We redefined our problem space once again to travel budgeting. For many college students, budget is the limiting factor for everything when it comes to traveling (flights, hotels, etc.). Students in particular don’t really have a disposable income, so their budget is smaller and becomes a real obstacle to traveling.


User Research

At the beginning of our research process, we began conducting standard interviews to better understand people’s general travel planning process. As we were able to gather data on people’s travel planning process, we also took note of our interviewee’s ages, sources of income, and how they thought about their trip in relation to money. Initially, we assumed that people our age, college students and recent graduates, were primarily budget travelers and had little money to spend on traveling. Yet, through our data we found that this was not the only case. Though our primary audience consisted of young adults, their access to money varied. Most people described themselves as the “broke, college student.” Traveling was something they’d want to do, but it was limited because they either have little to no source of income or rely on an allowance from their parents. In one specific case, one college student mentioned that his group wanted to travel to Japan but realized it would be too much so opted for traveling to Seattle, a cheaper option within the United States. When interviewing recent graduates who already had jobs, we found that they were more willing and eager to travel because of their stable source of income. They didn’t have to rely on their parent’s money or heavily consider their other friend’s budgets either. Their want to travel was more concrete and reasonable because they had the means to do so. Yet, they were still budget-minded since their income didn’t allow for excessive spending as they still had to pay for bills and student loans. A less common case we found, though still significant, was that some people who were still in college were somewhat budget-conscious when thinking about traveling, but it was not a huge limiting factor. Their parents were more than willing to spend for their travels, within reason, but since they were still spending their parent’s money, they weren’t looking into luxury spending. From this, we were able to create our three personas as described above.


Prototyping & Solution

Our first prototype of this solution focused on a budget planning solution that allowed users to visualize how much they’re spending on their trip. Our data revealed that young travelers keep their budget in mind, literally. The mentally store their budget, adding to an ever increasing amount of information they need to manage as they’re planning their travels. With our proposed solution, people would be provided a visual progress bar that allows them to be more conscious about their budget. Our budget “tracker” will be omnipresent throughout their planning process to guide them in making decision that are friendly to their budget. We imagined this tool would come in the form of a Chrome extension or another always-present option such as a Mac app. This tool would live near or on the platform our travelers used most for shopping for options, which most likely means their browser.

For our final prototype and solution, we included a budget estimator along with the visualizer, since many students did not know how much they should be spending or how much other people might spend on a trip. This budget estimator gives an average of how much other people spend overall and on certain categories of travel at different levels of spending profiles (low, middle, luxury). People are able to get a sense of how much they should be spending in comparison to others in order to get an idea of the amount they should set their budget at. Because we also observed that many people do not keep track of their expenses during their trip, and how much of their budget is being used, we would like to include a tool in future versions of our solution that will help with this issue as well. Other solutions we thought of were a payment plan for travel, similar to payment plans for a Coachella ticket. Students can book their trip months in advance and pay for their trip monthly instead of all at once. For example, a student would pay $100/mo. For 10 months for a trip to New York City rather than paying $1000 all at once.
